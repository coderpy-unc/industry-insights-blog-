At [ZeroVIR](https://www.zero-vir.com), the sale is not the end of our responsibility — it is the point at which our responsibility to the seller becomes most important. What happens to components after they leave the original seller's facility determines whether the seller's liability is genuinely closed or simply deferred to a future problem they don't yet know about.

This post explains what happens to components after ZeroVIR completes a purchase — and why that matters to the organisation that sold them.

## Why the post-sale chain matters to the seller

A seller who transfers inventory to a liquidator and receives payment might reasonably assume their responsibility ends there. In practice, it does not.

If components sold through a liquidator end up in a safety-critical application — an automotive control unit, a medical device, an aviation system — and fail in service, the supply chain trail is investigated. Regulators, legal teams, and product liability insurers follow the component from the point of failure back through every transaction to the original manufacturer or the entity that introduced the part into the secondary market.

If your components were genuine but were sold without authentication, commingled with counterfeits by a downstream broker, and then sold onward as verified — you are in that trail. Your name appears on the original transaction record. Proving that your parts were genuine at the time of sale requires documentation that informal liquidation channels do not produce.

This is not a theoretical risk. It is a documented litigation pattern in the electronics industry, particularly in automotive and aerospace component supply chains.

## What "authenticated buyer" means in practice

ZeroVIR sells exclusively to authenticated buyers — organisations that have been qualified through ZeroVIR's buyer onboarding process before their first purchase.

Buyer qualification covers: legal entity verification, registration in the relevant jurisdiction, declared end-use application and industry, and a review of the buyer's own quality management and authentication practices. We do not sell to intermediaries who intend to resell without further authentication. We do not sell into open marketplace listings where component provenance is obscured.

The buyer network covers: electronics manufacturing service companies building products for defined end markets, authorised repair and maintenance organisations, research institutions, and industrial equipment manufacturers with documented production programmes.

What this means for the seller: your components go to verified end users with legitimate applications, not into an undifferentiated grey market pool where the next step in the chain is unknown.

## The chain of custody documentation

Every ZeroVIR transaction generates a documented chain of custody that connects the original seller to the authenticated end buyer through ZeroVIR's authentication and qualification process. This chain is maintained in ZeroVIR's records and is available to the original seller on request.

The documentation the seller receives at transaction close:

**Certificate of Sale** — records the transaction in full: date, component description by manufacturer part number, quantity, lot number and date code, sale value, and a statement that the components have been transferred to ZeroVIR for authenticated resale. This is the document your finance team uses to close the inventory on your books and your compliance team uses to close the audit trail.

**Authentication Certificate** — confirms that the components were inspected and tested at an independent accredited laboratory and found to be genuine, within specification, and correctly represented by the stated part number and date code. This certificate is issued before any resale and is specific to the lot transferred.

**Certificate of Destruction** (where applicable) — for components that fail authentication or cannot be resold for other reasons (physical damage, obsolescence with no market demand), this certificate confirms that the components were destroyed and did not enter any supply chain. The destruction method and date are documented.

## What happens if components cannot be resold

Not all components that ZeroVIR purchases can be resold. Authentication failures, physical condition issues discovered during inspection, or market conditions that change between purchase and resale can result in components that are destroyed rather than sold.

In these cases, the seller's financial position is not affected — payment was made at the agreed price after inspection confirmed the inventory matched the submission. The Certificate of Destruction closes the trail.

This is meaningfully different from consignment arrangements, where unsellable components sit in the liquidator's warehouse indefinitely and the seller carries the uncertainty of when or whether they will be sold.

## Why ZeroVIR does not sell into open grey markets

The decision not to list on open grey market platforms is deliberate and structural, not a marketing positioning.

Open marketplace listings make the original seller visible. Part numbers, quantities, and date codes on an open listing allow anyone — including competitors, customers, and regulators — to infer the source. This is the confidentiality exposure we described in an earlier post on seller confidentiality.

Open market buyers are unqualified. ZeroVIR cannot control what happens to components after an anonymous buyer purchases them. Open market components can be relisted, commingled with other lots, sold without further authentication, and eventually reach safety-critical applications through chains that are untraceable.

Both of these create liability exposure for the original seller. ZeroVIR's closed buyer network is the structural response to both risks.
