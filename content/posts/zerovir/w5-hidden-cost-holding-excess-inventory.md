The decision to liquidate is almost always deferred longer than it should be — and the reason is almost always the same: the holding cost is invisible, and the optionality of keeping the parts feels real even when it isn't.

This post is for the CFO or finance director who wants to model this decision correctly.

## The carrying cost that nobody fully calculates

Most companies calculate inventory carrying cost as a simple storage figure — the cost per square metre of warehouse space multiplied by the volume of parts. This dramatically understates the true cost.

A complete carrying cost model for excess semiconductor inventory includes:

**Capital cost** — the opportunity cost of the cash tied up in inventory sitting on a shelf. At a typical corporate cost of capital of 8–12%, a ₹1 crore of excess semiconductor inventory costs ₹8–12 lakh per year in capital cost alone, before any other expense.

**Storage, insurance, and handling** — typically 2–4% of inventory value per year when correctly allocated.

**Obsolescence provision** — most finance teams apply a standard aging provision schedule. But semiconductor obsolescence does not follow a linear schedule. It accelerates. A component that has a 10% provision at 12 months may warrant a 60–70% provision at 36 months as the product generation moves on.

**Audit and compliance cost** — excess inventory that sits on the books at original cost requires periodic justification to auditors. The management time spent defending overvalued inventory has a real cost that never appears on an inventory carrying cost report.

Total carrying cost for semiconductor inventory, properly modelled: typically 15–25% of inventory value per year.

## The semiconductor price curve makes this worse

Unlike most physical inventory categories, semiconductor components experience predictable, systematic price decline. This is a structural feature of the semiconductor industry — manufacturing yields improve, capacity expands, and newer generations compete with older ones.

The average price decline for active semiconductor components is 10–15% per year. This is not a recession phenomenon or a market disruption — it is the normal operating condition of the industry, documented consistently across decades of semiconductor pricing data.

The practical implication: a component that you purchased at ₹100 per unit and hold for 3 years is worth approximately ₹60–70 per unit in the market, regardless of what it cost you. The carrying cost of 20% per year has compounded on top of an asset that itself declined by 30–40%.

A component held for 5 years at these rates is worth 40–50% of its original purchase price in real market terms — and that assumes it is still in demand at all.

## Why even 5% recovery is the right decision

The most common objection to early liquidation is the recovery rate. "They're only offering 10 cents on the dollar — I'll wait for a better offer."

This objection fails to account for two compounding realities.

**First: the asset is depreciating while you wait.** If you decline a 10% recovery offer today and wait 12 months, the component is now worth 10–15% less in the market. You have not preserved optionality — you have reduced the pool of value available to recover.

**Second: the probability that someone needs your specific component 5–10 years from now is approximately 1–2%.** Semiconductor components are specified into products with defined lifecycles. Once the product generation that used your component moves to end-of-life — which typically happens within 5–7 years — demand for that component is structurally gone. The market that might pay a premium for your parts does not exist in year 8.

This means that even a 5% recovery today is better than a 0% write-down in 5 years — mathematically, with certainty, in every scenario except the 1–2% chance that your specific component is needed by someone who cannot source it elsewhere during the holding period.

The decision model is simple: liquidate at whatever recovery rate the market offers today, invest the proceeds, and eliminate the carrying cost. The alternative — holding for a speculative future demand event — has a 98–99% chance of producing a worse outcome.

## The tax and audit dimension

Carrying inventory at original cost when market value has declined creates a balance sheet that auditors and tax authorities will eventually challenge. An impairment provision should be recognised when it becomes probable that the carrying amount will not be recovered — which in semiconductor inventory terms is typically within 2–3 years of excess identification.

Proactive liquidation, with a Certificate of Sale documenting the transaction, provides a clean write-down that closes the inventory on your books at documented realised value. This is significantly cleaner than a management-estimated impairment provision, and removes the risk of an auditor questioning the basis of your carrying value at year-end.
