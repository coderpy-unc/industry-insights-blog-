When a company decides to liquidate excess semiconductor inventory, the transaction itself is straightforward. The conversation nobody has is about what happens to your identity in that process.

Who knows you had the inventory? Who knows you sold it? Where does that information go?

At [ZeroVIR](https://www.zero-vir.com), confidentiality is not a feature we added to make sellers comfortable. It is a structural requirement of how responsible liquidation works. This post explains the specific reputational risks that come with selling excess inventory through channels that do not protect your identity.

## Why excess inventory is sensitive information

Excess semiconductor inventory does not appear in isolation. It is evidence of something that happened inside your business:

- A product line was cancelled or redesigned
- A customer cancelled a large order
- Procurement over-ordered during the shortage period and demand did not materialise
- A project was delayed and the components are now approaching obsolescence

None of these are unusual. All of them are competitively sensitive. A competitor who knows your exact excess inventory — part numbers, quantities, date codes — can infer a great deal about your product roadmap, your customer situation, and your supply chain decisions.

The inventory itself is not secret. The circumstances around it often are.

## What happens to your identity in an unprotected liquidation

When inventory moves through informal channels — a broker who lists it on an open marketplace, a trader who circulates your BOM to multiple potential buyers — your company name typically travels with the parts.

The listing may say "OEM excess — original packaging." The part numbers are searchable. Anyone who recognises the components and knows the market can make educated guesses about who the seller is. In a specialised segment — automotive PMICs, high-end SoCs, specific ARM MCUs — the universe of possible sellers is small. Your identity is not difficult to deduce.

Some brokers proactively use the seller's reputation to establish credibility with buyers — and it comes at your expense, without your permission.

## The specific risks

**Customer perception:** If a key customer discovers you are liquidating components associated with their project — even legitimately excess stock — it raises questions. Are deliveries at risk? Is the project being wound down?

**Competitor intelligence:** Part numbers reveal process nodes, design generations, and supplier relationships. A competitor systematically monitoring grey market listings can build a detailed picture of your design activity over time.

**Channel conflicts:** For authorised distributors, excess inventory appearing in unauthorised channels can trigger OEM audits and jeopardise franchise agreements — even if the sale itself was of genuine product.

**Employee and partner exposure:** Informal liquidation often involves discussions with multiple brokers before a deal is struck. Each conversation is a potential leak.
