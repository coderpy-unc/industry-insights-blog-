## The scale of the problem

Counterfeit electronic components are estimated to represent $75 billion or more in annual global electronics trade, with the volume growing as semiconductor supply chains become more complex and the secondary market expands. The industries most affected are automotive, aerospace, defence, industrial automation, and medical devices — sectors where a counterfeit component in a safety-critical position creates consequences that extend far beyond the cost of the part itself.

The US Department of Defense documented over 1 million suspect counterfeit parts in its supply chain in a single Senate Armed Services Committee investigation. Aviation authorities have documented counterfeit components reaching aircraft avionics. These are not edge cases — they represent a systemic problem in how electronic components move through global supply chains.

## The three entry points

Counterfeits enter the electronics supply chain through three primary channels:

**Grey market brokers operating without authentication.** The grey market for electronic components is legitimate — it exists to match supply and demand across geographies and time. The problem is not the grey market itself but the brokers within it who trade components without authentication. A broker who sources components from an unknown seller, tests nothing, and sells to a buyer who also tests nothing has created a pathway for counterfeits to travel from source to end user with no interception point. This is the most common entry pathway.

**Unauthorised distributors representing themselves as authorised.** Authorised distributors maintain chain-of-custody documentation from the original manufacturer. Unauthorised distributors — sometimes called "independent distributors" or "brokers" — may or may not have legitimate components. Some are reputable and maintain their own authentication processes. Many do not. The absence of manufacturer authorisation means there is no contractual obligation to authenticate, and no traceability back to the original source.

**Remarked and refurbished parts.** This is the most technically sophisticated counterfeit pathway. Used components — recovered from scrap PCBs, electronic waste, or salvage operations — are cleaned, resurfaced, and relabeled to appear as new, unused parts. The remarking process involves sanding or sandblasting the original marking off the component package, applying new markings with the original part number and a recent date code, and sometimes replating the leads to remove oxidation. The result is a component that passes visual inspection but may fail in circuit — either immediately or after a shorter-than-expected service life.

## How counterfeits are made — the technical detail

**Sanding and relabeling** — the most common remarking method for plastic-packaged ICs. The top surface of the package is sanded smooth, the original marking is removed, and a new marking is applied. Under magnification, sanded packages show characteristic surface texture different from factory-original packages. Under blacklight, remarking inks respond differently from original laser or ink-jet marking.

**BGA reballing** — ball grid array packages are removed from scrap PCBs, the solder balls are removed, and new balls are applied. The component is cleaned and sometimes remarketed as new. BGA reballing is difficult to detect visually but reveals itself under X-ray inspection — the ball size, uniformity, and internal void content differ from factory-new parts.

**Cloning** — the most sophisticated counterfeit type. A manufacturer in an unauthorised location produces a component that mimics the external appearance and sometimes the basic function of a genuine part. Clones may pass simple functional tests but fail under extended operation, temperature extremes, or radiation environments. Clones are detected through electrical parametric testing against manufacturer specifications, die analysis, and lot traceability.

## The four tests that catch counterfeits

**Visual inspection** — the first line of defence, and insufficient on its own. Trained inspectors examine package surfaces for sanding marks, laser remarking, misaligned or inconsistent fonts, incorrect date code formats, and surface finish inconsistencies. Visual inspection catches a significant percentage of remarked parts but misses clones and high-quality remarks entirely.

**X-ray inspection** — reveals internal construction. Genuine parts from a specific manufacturer have consistent internal bond wire geometry, die size, and package construction. Counterfeits — particularly reballed BGAs and clones — show deviations from the expected internal construction. X-ray inspection is particularly important for BGAs, QFNs, and other packages where internal construction cannot be visually assessed.

**Electrical testing** — parametric testing against the manufacturer's datasheet verifies that the component meets its specified electrical characteristics: voltage thresholds, current ratings, timing parameters, leakage currents, and functional operation. A component that passes visual and X-ray inspection but fails parametric testing is either a clone or a failed genuine part being sold as working.

**Lot traceability** — verification that the lot number, date code, and country of origin on the component package are consistent with the manufacturer's production records for that part number. Manufacturers of major ICs maintain traceability databases, and some provide direct verification services. Lot traceability is the only test that can positively confirm a component's origin rather than simply ruling out obvious counterfeits.

## How this connects to excess inventory liquidation

If your genuine components are sold without authentication into the secondary market, they can be mixed with counterfeits by a downstream broker. If those mixed lots later cause a failure in a safety-critical application, tracing the counterfeit back through the supply chain to your liquidation event — even though your parts were genuine — creates reputational and legal exposure.
