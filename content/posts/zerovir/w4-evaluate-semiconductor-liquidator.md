At [ZeroVIR](https://www.zero-vir.com), we are regularly approached by OEMs and EMS companies that have been burned by a previous liquidation experience — a below-market offer accepted because they had no benchmark, a confidentiality breach discovered indirectly, or payment terms that stretched from 7 days to 90 days with no warning.

The liquidation market for electronic components has low barriers to entry and limited regulation. A firm can operate as a broker with nothing more than a trade directory listing and an email address. Evaluating who you are dealing with before committing your inventory is not bureaucratic caution — it is basic commercial risk management.

These five questions provide a practical framework.

## Question 1: Do you buy inventory outright, or work on consignment?

This is the first and most important question. The answer determines your financial exposure and timeline to recovery.

An outright buyer commits to a price and pays on a defined schedule after inspection. Your inventory is off your balance sheet on a known date. The risk of finding an end buyer sits with the liquidator.

Consignment means the liquidator sells on your behalf and remits proceeds after sale. Your inventory stays on your balance sheet. You receive payment only when the components sell — weeks, months, or never, depending on demand.

Serious liquidators with capital buy outright. Brokers with limited balance sheets default to consignment.

ZeroVIR purchases outright. The offer is a firm number, not a range. Payment terms are in the purchase order before any inventory moves.

## Question 2: What is your authentication process, and who performs it?

Counterfeit electronic components are a documented, growing problem in the secondary market. Without authentication, your genuine components can be mixed with counterfeits — and if those parts end up in a safety-critical application, the original seller can face liability even if their parts were genuine.

Authentication must cover: visual and dimensional inspection, X-ray verification of internal construction, electrical testing to manufacturer datasheet parameters, and lot traceability verification.

Critical follow-up: who performs it? In-house testing at the liquidator's own facility is weaker than testing at an independent ISO 17025 or IDEA-STD-1010 accredited laboratory. If they cannot name the laboratory and the specific test methods — they are not authenticating. They are grading visually and selling on that basis.

## Question 3: What are your confidentiality commitments, and are they in writing?

Your part numbers, quantities, and the circumstances of the liquidation reveal your product roadmap, customer situation, and supply chain decisions to anyone paying attention in the market. A credible liquidator commits to confidentiality before reviewing your inventory list — not after.

The commitment must be specific: your company name will not appear in any listing, marketing material, or third-party communication. The buyer of your components will not know who the original seller was. These commitments survive the close of the transaction.

Ask for this in writing before sharing any inventory data. A liquidator unwilling to commit in writing before seeing your parts is telling you something important about how they operate.

## Question 4: What is your payment timeline and structure?

Standard practice: payment within 7–14 days of inspection confirming the inventory matches the submission. Bank transfer. Timeline specified in the purchase order.

The common deviation: terms that expand significantly after the inventory has moved to the liquidator's facility and your negotiating position has weakened. Terms agreed at submission should be terms honoured at settlement.

For high-value transactions, escrow through a neutral third party protects both sides. Funds are held and released upon confirmed inspection and handover. If a liquidator is unwilling to consider escrow for large transactions, note it.

## Question 5: Can you provide references and a certificate example?

The certificate of sale is the audit document your finance and compliance teams need to close the inventory on your books. Ask for a sanitised example before committing. It should show: transaction date, component description, quantity, value, and confirmation of authenticated buyer. For scrapped components, a certificate of destruction confirms they did not enter the market.

References from previous OEM or EMS sellers give you direct evidence of how the liquidator operates in practice. A liquidator with a track record can provide them.

If they cannot provide either — they are operating informally. The risk in an informal transaction is yours.
