You have MCUs sitting in a warehouse. A project got cancelled. A redesign made them obsolete. The procurement team placed a double-order during the 2021 shortage and half of it never got consumed.

This is not unusual. Industry estimates put the global excess semiconductor inventory at somewhere between $50–100 billion at the peak of the post-pandemic glut — and the automotive and industrial segments are still working through it.

The question is what to do with it.

Most companies stall. They keep the parts on the books at full value, hoping demand returns. It rarely does — and while they wait, the components age, the market price erodes, and the write-down eventually happens anyway.

Professional liquidation is the alternative. Here is what that process actually looks like.

## Step 1 — Submit your inventory list

The process starts with you sending a detailed BOM-style inventory list. This should include:

- Part numbers (manufacturer PN, not internal codes)
- Quantity available
- Date codes and lot numbers where known
- Original manufacturer
- Physical condition and storage history

**We only accept Tier 1 active components** — MCUs, PMICs, MOSFETs, SoCs, ARM processors, DDR/LPDDR memory, and similar high-value active ICs. We do not handle passives, connectors, or mechanical parts.

We also do not accept rusted, physically damaged, or improperly stored components. Our model is built around **obsolete and aged — but intact — inventory**. Parts that have been sitting in a climate-controlled warehouse for three years are exactly what we deal with. Parts that have been sitting on a loading dock are not.

Confidentiality is standard at this stage. At [ZeroVIR](https://www.zero-vir.com), we guarantee this in writing before the inventory list is even reviewed.

## Step 2 — Valuation and offer

Within 100 hours of receiving your list, you receive a firm offer — not an estimate range, a number.

The valuation is based on:

- **Current market demand** for each part number
- **Date codes** — fresher stock commands better prices
- **Lot integrity** — full reels and sealed trays vs. partial quantities
- **Alternative sourcing** — if the same PN is available elsewhere, it depresses the offer

High-value active components — automotive PMICs, ARM MCUs, SoCs, Power MOSFETs — retain value well.

## Step 3 — Agreement and purchase order

Once the offer is accepted, a formal purchase agreement is issued followed by a purchase order. Payment terms are specified upfront, with a target settlement within 7 days of inspection completion.

For large-value transactions, we support **escrow settlement** — funds are held by a neutral third party and released upon confirmed inspection and handover. This protects both sides and is increasingly the preferred structure for deals above a certain threshold.

No consignment. No "we'll pay you when we sell it."

## Step 4 — Pickup and inspection

Collection is arranged at no cost to you through **reputed third-party logistics partners**, ensuring full chain-of-custody documentation and traceable paperwork from your facility to the inspection lab. The shipment is insured and tracked end-to-end.

The components are then inspected and authenticated at a certified lab. Authentication covers:

- Visual and dimensional inspection
- X-ray for internal construction verification
- Electrical testing against manufacturer specifications
- Lot traceability checks

## Step 5 — Payment and certification

Payment is released after inspection confirms the inventory matches what was submitted. You receive two documents:

- **Certificate of sale** — audit-proof record for your finance and compliance teams
- **Certificate of destruction** (if applicable) — for parts scrapped, confirming they did not enter the market

The certificates matter. Finance teams need them for write-off documentation. Quality teams need them to close the inventory in their systems.

## Why this matters now

More than 750,000 electronic components reached end-of-life in 2022 alone — a year-over-year increase exceeding 40%. The pace has not slowed. Every quarter, more part numbers are discontinued, more redesigns are triggered, and more inventory becomes stranded.

The 7-day process exists because speed matters. Every week a component sits in your warehouse costs you in storage, insurance, and opportunity cost — while the market price moves against you.
