## What a lead-acid battery actually costs you

Most buyers compare the purchase price of a lead-acid battery against a lithium battery and stop there. This comparison misses most of the cost.

**Water top-up** — a flooded lead-acid battery loses water through electrolysis during every charge cycle. In Indian conditions — high ambient temperatures, frequent cycling during power cuts — this means topping up with distilled water every 30–45 days. Distilled water costs money. More importantly, it costs time, and incorrect water levels (too low or too high) accelerate degradation. A battery that misses water top-ups consistently will fail years ahead of its rated life.

**Capacity loss from the first year** — lead-acid batteries do not hold their rated capacity over their lifetime. A new 150Ah battery delivers approximately 130–140Ah in its first year. By year 2, you are typically at 110–120Ah. By year 3, 90–100Ah — 60–65% of the original rating. The backup time you measured when the battery was new is no longer what you get. The degradation is gradual and invisible until the backup time becomes noticeably short.

**The 50% DoD reality** — this is the most misunderstood aspect of lead-acid battery specifications. Lead-acid cells suffer accelerated degradation when discharged below 50% of their capacity. A 150Ah lead-acid battery has a safe usable capacity of approximately 75Ah — half the rated figure. If you discharge it to 80% depth (common during extended power cuts), you are damaging the battery on every such cycle. At 50% DoD, a lead-acid battery is rated for 300–500 cycles. Frequent deeper discharges dramatically reduce this.

**Replacement at 2–3 years** — in India, where power cuts are frequent and batteries cycle daily, a lead-acid battery doing one full cycle per day reaches its rated cycle limit in 1–1.5 years. In practice, considering the DoD damage from extended outages, most Indian households replace their lead-acid battery every 2–3 years.

**Maintenance and acid risk** — beyond water topping, lead-acid batteries require periodic terminal cleaning (corrosion is inevitable), equalisation charging (a deliberate overcharge to prevent stratification), and careful handling to avoid acid spills. Acid fumes are corrosive to nearby metal surfaces and harmful if inhaled. These are not theoretical risks — they are routine realities of lead-acid battery ownership.
