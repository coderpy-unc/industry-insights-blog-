The financing landscape for EV and solar in India is genuinely complex: multiple schemes at central and state levels, each with different eligibility criteria, different disbursement mechanisms, and different timelines. What follows is a practical map of the major schemes as they stand in 2026.

## FAME II — Electric Vehicle Subsidies

The Faster Adoption and Manufacturing of Electric Vehicles scheme (FAME II) is the central government's primary demand-side incentive for EV adoption. The scheme has gone through multiple revisions since its 2019 launch, with eligibility criteria and subsidy amounts adjusted periodically.

**Current coverage (2026):** FAME II subsidies are available for electric two-wheelers, three-wheelers, buses, and — in specific categories — light commercial vehicles. Passenger cars were removed from FAME II eligibility in an earlier revision and remain excluded.

**Subsidy structure for two and three-wheelers:** The subsidy is calculated per kWh of battery capacity, subject to a cap as a percentage of the ex-factory vehicle price. For electric two-wheelers, the subsidy is approximately ₹10,000–15,000 per vehicle depending on battery size. For electric three-wheelers (e-rickshaws and e-carts), subsidy amounts vary by state implementation.

**Critical requirement:** FAME II subsidies are only available on vehicles from OEMs that have received type approval and are registered on the FAME II portal. Vehicles from non-registered OEMs — including many lower-cost e-rickshaw assemblers — do not qualify regardless of the buyer's eligibility. Verify OEM registration before assuming subsidy availability.

**Disbursement:** FAME II subsidies are disbursed at the point of purchase through the registered dealer, reducing the upfront vehicle cost directly. The buyer does not need to file a separate claim — the dealer processes the subsidy through the FAME II portal.

## PM-KUSUM — Solar for Agriculture

The Pradhan Mantri Kisan Urja Suraksha evam Utthan Mahabhiyan (PM-KUSUM) scheme supports solar energy adoption in the agricultural sector across three components:

**Component A** — installation of decentralised grid-connected solar power plants of capacity 500 kW to 2 MW on barren or uncultivable land by individual farmers, cooperatives, or panchayats. The power generated is sold to DISCOMs at a fixed tariff. Central and state government subsidies cover up to 60% of the plant cost, with the balance financed through bank loans or farmer equity.

**Component B** — standalone solar-powered agriculture pumps replacing diesel pumps. Subsidy covers 60% of the benchmark cost, with states often adding additional subsidy. This component is highly relevant for farmers with unelectrified fields or unreliable grid power.

**Component C** — solarisation of existing grid-connected agriculture pumps. A solar panel system is installed that powers the pump during daylight hours, with excess generation exported to the grid. The farmer receives a buyback tariff on exported units, partially offsetting the system cost.

**Access:** PM-KUSUM implementation varies significantly by state. Contact the state nodal agency (DISCOM or state renewable energy development agency) to confirm current allocation availability, as component-wise funding is released in tranches.

## MNRE Rooftop Solar Scheme

The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy's rooftop solar subsidy programme supports residential and institutional solar installations up to 10 kW.

**Current subsidy structure:** For residential systems up to 3 kW, the central subsidy is ₹18,000 per kW. For systems between 3 kW and 10 kW, the subsidy is ₹18,000 per kW for the first 3 kW and ₹9,000 per kW for the remainder. Beyond 10 kW, central subsidy is not available, though state schemes may apply.

**Net metering:** Residential rooftop solar systems are eligible for net metering under state electricity regulations, allowing excess generation to be exported to the grid and offset against consumption. Net metering tariffs and banking provisions vary by state.

**Access:** Applications are processed through the PM Surya Ghar portal (pmsuryaghar.gov.in). The applicant selects an empanelled vendor, the system is installed, and the subsidy is disbursed directly to the applicant's bank account after inspection and commissioning approval by the DISCOM.

## Bank and NBFC Financing for EV Fleets

For commercial EV fleet operators — e-rickshaw fleets, delivery vehicle fleets, electric three-wheeler cargo operators — dedicated financing products are available from several institutions:

**SIDBI (Small Industries Development Bank of India)** offers direct financing for EV purchases and EV fleet charging infrastructure under its clean energy financing programmes. Interest rates are typically below commercial bank rates, with loan tenors matched to the expected vehicle lifecycle.

**State Bank of India, Bank of Baroda, and several NBFCs** have dedicated EV loan products with features including lower interest rates versus conventional vehicle loans, extended repayment periods (up to 5 years for commercial EVs), and in some cases moratorium periods aligned with the early operational phase of the fleet.

**Fleet financing:** For operators purchasing 10 or more vehicles, structured fleet financing with bulk discount pricing from OEMs and customised repayment terms from lending partners is increasingly available. Green Eco India facilitates introductions to financing partners for fleet customers.

## State-Level Schemes

Several Indian states have EV and solar policies with additional incentives beyond the central scheme:

**Delhi EV Policy** offers additional purchase incentives for electric two-wheelers, three-wheelers, and goods vehicles registered in Delhi, road tax exemption, and priority registration. The scheme has been extended multiple times and remains active in 2026.

**Maharashtra** offers additional purchase subsidy for EVs, waiver of registration fees, and reduced electricity tariff for EV charging. The state also has a dedicated EV charging infrastructure policy with subsidy for charging point installation.

**Gujarat** has one of India's more comprehensive state EV policies, with purchase incentives, exemption from road tax and registration fees, and subsidy for public charging infrastructure. Gujarat's manufacturing incentives for EV component producers have also attracted BMS and battery pack manufacturers to the state.

## Navigating the Process

The gap between a scheme existing and a customer successfully accessing it is significant. Common failure points include: OEM not registered on the FAME II portal, DISCOM not processing net metering applications in a timely manner, state nodal agency allocation exhausted before application is processed, and documentation requirements not met at the time of application.
