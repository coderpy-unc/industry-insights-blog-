Most insulation failures on Indian construction projects are not material failures. They are specification failures.

The wrong density gets supplied. The facing is omitted. The IS standard is not called out. The contractor prices against the cheapest available product — and that is what gets installed. Six months later, the HVAC system is underperforming, the roof is sweating, and nobody can agree on whose fault it is.

[Sipla Solutions](https://www.siplasolutions.com) has been in the insulation supply chain since 1994. This post covers exactly how to write a glasswool specification in a tender that leaves no room for substitution.

## The 2024 revision of IS 8183 — what changed

IS 8183 is India's standard for bonded mineral wool. It was revised in 2024 for the first time since 1993.

Key changes in IS 8183:2024:

- **New bulk density group added** — expanding the range of standardised densities
- **Tighter dimensional tolerances** — reducing variation in thickness and width
- **Reduced sulphur content limits** — important for corrosion control near metal structures
- **Expanded thermal conductivity specifications** — more precise performance benchmarking

When writing a tender specification today, always reference **IS 8183:2024**. Any supplier worth engaging will provide test certificates against the 2024 revision.

## The five things every glasswool specification must include

**1. Density — in kg/m³**

This is the single most important parameter. Density directly determines thermal and acoustic performance. Do not write "glasswool as per IS 8183" without specifying density.

Common densities and their applications:

- **16 kg/m³** — light roof insulation, non-AC sheds
- **24 kg/m³** — standard PEB under-deck, HVAC ducts
- **32 kg/m³** — higher acoustic performance, partition walls
- **48 kg/m³** — acoustic panels, AHU lining, studio treatment

Write it explicitly: *"Glasswool, 24 kg/m³ density, conforming to IS 8183:2024"*

**2. Thickness — in mm**

Thermal resistance increases with thickness. Specify the exact thickness required. Common specifications:

- 50mm for standard PEB roof insulation
- 75mm–100mm for higher thermal resistance
- 25mm for duct lining

Do not write "adequate thickness." Write the number.

**3. Facing — type and position**

Common facings:

- **FSK (Aluminium Foil + Scrim + Kraft)** — standard for PEB roofs, reflects radiant heat
- **Glass tissue** — acoustic applications, AHU lining
- **Vinyl facing** — where aesthetics matter, partition walls

Specify both the facing type and which face it goes on.

**4. IS 8183:2024 conformance — explicitly stated**

Write: *"Material shall conform to IS 8183:2024 and supplier shall provide BIS-certified test certificates on request."*

Without this, a contractor can supply uncertified material and argue it meets the specification because no standard was named.

**5. Test certificates — requested upfront**

Add to the specification: *"Supplier to provide thermal conductivity test certificate and density test certificate from a NABL-accredited laboratory prior to material delivery."*

This eliminates the possibility of substitution after award.

## A sample specification line

For a standard PEB under-deck application:

*"Supply and fix glasswool insulation blanket, 24 kg/m³ density, 50mm thickness, with FSK facing on top surface, conforming to IS 8183:2024. Supplier to provide NABL-accredited test certificates for thermal conductivity and density prior to material delivery to site."*

This single line closes every common substitution loophole.

## What happens when the specification is vague

A vague specification — "glasswool insulation as required" — is an invitation for the lowest-cost substitution. In practice this means lower density, no facing, and no test certificates — making performance verification impossible after installation.

The cost difference between correct and incorrect glasswool on a 10,000 sq ft PEB roof is significant. The cost of HVAC overcapacity to compensate for poor insulation over 20 years is far larger.
