Fiberglass tissue is one of the most misspecified materials in the composites supply chain, often confused with woven roving or chopped strand mat by procurement teams who treat them as interchangeable. They are not.

## What fiberglass tissue is — and what it isn't

Fiberglass tissue is a nonwoven mat of very fine glass fibres, bonded together with a chemical binder, produced in weights typically ranging from 20 g/m² to 100 g/m². It is not a structural reinforcement. It does not contribute meaningfully to the tensile or flexural strength of a laminate.

What it does do, precisely and cost-effectively, is control the surface of a composite part. The two distinct products in this category serve different purposes:

**Surface tissue (overlay mat)** — applied as the outermost layer of an FRP laminate, directly behind the gel coat or on the exterior face of a panel. Its primary function is to provide a resin-rich, pinhole-free surface that resists osmosis and provides a smooth, paintable finish. Surface tissue also acts as a barrier to print-through — the tendency of the underlying woven or chopped strand mat reinforcement pattern to telegraph through the outer surface as the laminate cures and the resin shrinks.

**Overlay tissue (chopped strand overlay)** — applied between structural layers of the laminate to improve interlaminar adhesion, smooth out resin distribution between layers of different architecture (e.g., woven roving over CSM), and reduce dry spots and voids at layer interfaces.

Confusing the two — or specifying one where the other is needed — produces either a surface finish problem or a laminate integrity problem. Neither is cheap to fix after the part is cured.

## Primary applications in India

**FRP pipes and tanks** — surface tissue is used as the innermost layer of chemical-resistant pipe and tank laminates, providing a chemically resistant resin-rich surface in contact with the process fluid. The tissue layer protects the structural glass reinforcement from direct chemical attack. For this application, tissue compatible with vinyl ester or bisphenol epoxy resin systems is typically specified — standard polyester-compatible tissue is not adequate for aggressive chemical service.

**Boat hulls and marine structures** — surface tissue behind the gel coat is standard practice in quality boat construction. It prevents print-through from the underlying woven roving, provides an osmosis barrier, and gives the gel coat a uniform backing surface. Marine-grade tissue must have very low binder content to allow full resin wet-out and must be compatible with the polyester or epoxy resin system in use.

**Wind turbine blade components** — overlay tissue between layers of biaxial and triaxial fabrics in blade laminates improves interlaminar shear strength and reduces dry-spot formation in large-format vacuum infusion layups. The weight and binder chemistry must be compatible with epoxy infusion resin systems.

**Building panels and cladding** — surface tissue provides the smooth surface finish on GRP flat and corrugated sheets. The tissue controls resin content at the surface, reducing waste and providing a consistent gel coat or paint adhesion surface.

## Key specifications — what to call out

When specifying fiberglass tissue for an FRP application, five parameters must be explicitly stated:

**Weight (g/m²)** — the most fundamental specification. Lighter tissue (25–30 g/m²) provides less surface build and is used for thin laminates and gel coat backing. Heavier tissue (50–100 g/m²) provides more surface build and is used where print-through resistance and chemical barrier performance are primary requirements.

**Binder type** — this is the most critical and most frequently underspecified parameter. Fiberglass tissue binders are typically polyester, epoxy-compatible, or universal. A polyester binder tissue used in an epoxy laminate will dissolve partially during cure — the binder chemistry is incompatible with the resin system, causing delamination at the tissue layer. Always confirm binder compatibility with the resin system before procurement.

**Resin compatibility** — specify the resin system explicitly: polyester, vinyl ester, or epoxy. The supplier must confirm compatibility. Do not assume compatibility based on price or availability.

**Tensile strength (dry and wet)** — relevant for handling during layup. Tissue that tears during application in a wet layup process slows production and creates defects. Minimum dry tensile strength of 100 N/5cm is typical for production applications.

**IS/EN conformance** — EN 13471 covers glass fibre reinforced plastics — tissue and lightweight glass fibre reinforcement materials. For Indian procurement, request conformance testing data against this standard or the equivalent ASTM D1668 where applicable.

## The binder problem — why cheap tissue delammates

The most common fiberglass tissue failure in Indian FRP fabrication is interlaminar delamination at the tissue layer. In the majority of cases, the root cause is binder incompatibility.

Low-cost tissue imported from certain sources uses a powder binder that is optimised for polyester resin systems. When this tissue is used — as it often is, without specification checks — in vinyl ester or epoxy laminates, the binder is not fully dissolved by the resin during cure. The result is a partially bonded interface between the tissue and the adjacent laminate layer. Under service loads, thermal cycling, or chemical exposure, this interface fails.

The fix is straightforward: require the supplier to provide a resin compatibility certificate, not just a weight and binder type declaration.
