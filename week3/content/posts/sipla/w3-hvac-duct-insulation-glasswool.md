At [Sipla Solutions](https://www.siplasolutions.com), we supply glass wool and insulation products to HVAC contractors and MEP consultants across India. Duct insulation is one of the most common applications — and one where specification errors are surprisingly frequent.

This guide covers how glass wool is correctly specified and installed for HVAC ducts in Indian conditions — including two failure causes that are almost never mentioned in standard insulation guides but matter enormously in Indian installations: moisture ingress and rodent damage.

## Why duct insulation matters more in India than most markets

HVAC ducts carry conditioned air — typically 12°C to 16°C supply air — through spaces that may be at 40°C or higher. Without insulation, two problems arise simultaneously.

**Thermal loss**: The temperature differential between the duct surface and ambient air causes heat gain into the supply air. In a poorly insulated system, a significant portion of the cooling capacity is absorbed before the air reaches the supply diffuser. The chiller works harder, energy costs rise, and comfort suffers.

**Condensation**: When a cold duct surface contacts humid ambient air, moisture condenses on the outer surface. In Indian monsoon conditions — where relative humidity can exceed 90% — an uninsulated or under-insulated duct will drip condensate continuously, damaging ceilings and causing mould growth.

## Glass wool product selection for ducts

For HVAC duct applications in India, glass wool is used in two primary forms:

**Flexible glass wool blanket with aluminium foil facing** — the standard product for rectangular and round ducts in commercial buildings. Typical specification: 25mm thick, 24 kg/m³ density, with factory-bonded reinforced aluminium foil facing.

**Rigid glass wool board with foil facing** — used where mechanical protection or a cleaner finish is required, such as exposed duct runs in lobbies or server rooms. Boards at 48 kg/m³ are standard for this application.

For AHU lining, higher-density glass wool boards at 48 kg/m³ to 64 kg/m³ are used, with a glass tissue facing to prevent fibre erosion from the air stream.

## Moisture control — the most critical factor in Indian installations

In India's climate, vapour control is not a secondary concern — it is the primary determinant of whether an insulation system performs over its intended life.

The aluminium foil facing on glass wool duct wrap is the vapour barrier. If it is punctured, torn, or if joints are left without tape, moist ambient air will migrate into the insulation and condense within the glass wool itself. Wet insulation loses most of its thermal resistance and creates conditions for sustained mould growth inside the insulation layer — invisible from the outside, but progressive.

**What correct moisture control looks like in practice:**

- All longitudinal seams must be positioned on the side of the duct, never the bottom — bottom seams are more likely to open under gravity and allow condensate pooling
- Every seam and joint must be sealed with aluminium foil tape — not masking tape, not duct tape, and not left relying on overlap alone
- All penetrations — hangers, supports, sensor wires, access panels — must be sealed before insulation is applied
- Insulation must not be compressed at support points; a 25mm blanket compressed to 12mm at a hanger provides roughly half the designed thermal resistance at that location, creating a cold bridge where condensation reliably forms

In high-humidity locations — exposed risers, plant rooms, rooftop ducts — specify 38mm to 50mm thickness rather than the standard 25mm. The extra thickness keeps the outer surface temperature above the dew point even on the most humid monsoon days.

Any insulation repair must restore the vapour barrier, not just the insulation layer. A patch of glass wool without foil replacement is not a repair — it is a future moisture problem.

## Rodent control — a uniquely Indian challenge

This is rarely discussed in insulation specifications, but in Indian commercial and industrial buildings, rodent damage to duct insulation is a genuine maintenance problem. Rats and mice chew through glass wool blankets — particularly at seams, joints, and exposed edges — to nest inside the warm, soft material. Once an insulation system is breached in this way, the vapour barrier is compromised, moisture ingress begins, and the thermal performance degrades rapidly.

Standard glass wool duct wrap offers no inherent rodent resistance. In buildings where rodent activity is known or likely — which in India means most industrial sites, food processing facilities, warehouses, and older commercial buildings — additional measures are necessary:

**Aluminium cladding on exposed duct runs**: A 0.5mm to 0.8mm aluminium sheet cladding over the insulation is the most effective barrier. It eliminates rodent access entirely on the clad sections. This should be standard specification for all ducts in plant rooms, service areas, and ceiling voids in food or pharma facilities.

**Metal mesh reinforced facing**: Some glass wool products are available with wire mesh or metal-reinforced facing that provides mechanical resistance to gnawing. Specify this for ducts at lower levels or in service corridors where rodent exposure is higher.

**Sealed terminations**: Rodents typically enter insulation at cut ends and unsealed joints. All cut edges should be sealed with aluminium tape and termination collars around supports. This simple step eliminates the most common entry points.

**Coordination with pest control**: Insulation cannot substitute for a pest control programme in a rodent-prone building. The correct approach is to address both — pest control to manage the population, and robust insulation specification to eliminate the nesting opportunity that makes duct runs attractive.

## Common specification errors to avoid

**Under-specifying density**: Using 16 kg/m³ glass wool for duct wrap — a common cost-cutting substitution — results in a blanket that compresses easily and fails to maintain contact with the duct surface. The minimum for HVAC duct wrap in India is 24 kg/m³.

**Omitting the vapour barrier**: Specifying glass wool without aluminium foil facing for duct applications in humid Indian conditions is not a cost saving — it is a guaranteed future failure.

**No tape at joints**: Many installations use no tape at seams, relying on overlap alone. In high-humidity conditions, this is insufficient and will allow moisture ingress within one to two monsoon seasons.
