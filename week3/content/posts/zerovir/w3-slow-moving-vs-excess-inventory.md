At [ZeroVIR](https://www.zero-vir.com), we work with OEMs, EMS providers, and distributors managing surplus electronic component inventory. One pattern we see repeatedly: companies wait too long to act because they have misclassified the problem.

Slow-moving inventory and excess inventory look similar on a stock report. They are fundamentally different in terms of risk, recovery options, and the right course of action.

## The definitions matter

**Slow-moving inventory** is stock that is consuming more holding time than normal but is still actively used in production. The forecast says it will clear — just more slowly than planned. The stock is current, the component is in the active BOM, and the issue is a demand shortfall or production delay.

**Excess inventory** is stock that exceeds foreseeable demand within the component's useful commercial life. This includes: components for products that have been cancelled or redesigned, stock ordered in excess due to forecast error, and components that have been superseded by a new revision or substitute part.

Slow-moving inventory is a cash flow problem. Excess inventory is a write-down waiting to happen.

## Why the misclassification happens

Inventory classification systems in most ERP platforms flag stock by days-on-hand or turnover ratio. A component sitting for 180 days triggers the same alert whether it is a slow mover on an active product or genuine excess from a cancelled programme.

Procurement teams, under pressure to avoid shortages, tend to reclassify excess as slow-moving. The logic is: "We might need it eventually." This is psychologically comfortable and financially dangerous.

The practical consequence: excess inventory accumulates on the balance sheet, unchallenged, until an audit or product end-of-life forces a write-down. By that point, the market value of the components may have deteriorated substantially — particularly for semiconductors where newer generations have replaced the part.

## The time value of semiconductor inventory

Electronic components, and semiconductors in particular, depreciate differently from most physical inventory. Their value is not primarily a function of physical condition — it is a function of market availability and demand.

A component in excess today may still have significant market value, because the broader market may also be short or constrained. The same component in 18 months may be worth a fraction of today's price, because the market will have rebalanced, newer alternatives will have been adopted, or the products that used it will have reached end-of-life across the industry.

This is the fundamental reason why the timing of excess inventory disposition matters. Acting early, when the component is still in active production at the OEM level and the market demand base is intact, produces substantially better recovery outcomes than acting after the product cycle has turned.

## Identifying which category your stock falls into

A practical approach to reclassification uses three questions:

**Is this component still in the active BOM of a production programme?** If yes, and the production programme is ongoing, the stock is slow-moving and the priority is to reforecast demand and adjust future purchasing.

**What is the end-of-life horizon for the products that use this component?** If the product is within 12 months of planned end-of-life, the component should be reclassified as excess regardless of whether it is on an active BOM — because the remaining demand is insufficient to clear the stock.

**Has the component been superseded or does a preferred substitute exist?** If engineering has already moved to a new revision or alternative part, the original component is functionally excess even if it has not been formally flagged.

## What to do with confirmed excess

Once a component is correctly identified as excess, the options are: consume it through modified production planning, return it to the distributor or manufacturer under any applicable stock rotation rights, or liquidate it through the secondary market.

For most excess semiconductor inventory, secondary market liquidation is the practical route — distributor stock rotation rights are usually limited in scope and time, and production programme modifications to consume excess inventory often create more cost than the stock is worth.
