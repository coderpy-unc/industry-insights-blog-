The robotics industry has produced an enormous volume of content explaining why robots are ready for enterprise deployment. The hardware works. The AI has improved. The ROI case, on paper, is compelling.

Enterprises already know this. Most large hospitality groups, healthcare operators, logistics companies, and food producers have evaluated robotics. Many have run pilots. The question is not whether robots work in a demo environment. The question is why full-scale enterprise deployments remain rare.

At [Sirona Robotics](https://www.sironarobotics.com), we spend significant time understanding the real barriers — because solving them is the actual business of deploying robotic workforce infrastructure, as opposed to selling hardware and leaving integration to someone else. Here is what we consistently find.

## Barrier 1: The integration cost exceeds the robot cost

Enterprise operations run on systems that have been built, customised, and integrated over years — sometimes decades. ERP, PMS, LIMS, WMS, access control, HR systems. These systems are not standardised. They have custom fields, custom workflows, and integration points that are undocumented or documented only in the memory of the team that built them.

When an enterprise evaluates a robotics vendor, they receive a hardware cost, a software licence cost, and sometimes an integration estimate. That integration estimate is almost always understated — because the vendor does not know the full complexity of the enterprise's system landscape until they are inside the project.

The actual integration cost in complex enterprise environments frequently equals or exceeds the hardware cost. Enterprises that have run pilots know this. It is a primary reason why a successful pilot does not convert to a full deployment: the pilot was run in a controlled environment with limited integration, and the cost of the full integration was not anticipated at the decision stage.

**What resolves this:** A vendor that owns the integration layer — not a system integrator hired by the customer, not a professional services firm brought in post-sale. The robotics vendor must maintain connectors to the platforms their customers actually use: major PMS platforms for hospitality, LIMS platforms for labs, SAP and Oracle for enterprise ERP. When the enterprise's platform is updated, the connector is the vendor's problem to maintain, not the customer's.

## Barrier 2: Change management is underestimated — and under-resourced

A robot does not replace a process. It changes a process. Housekeeping workflows, lab sample transit procedures, warehouse picking patterns — all of these need to be redesigned around what the robot can and cannot do, and what the human now does differently as a result.

This is not a technology problem. It is a people and process problem. And most robotics vendors have no competence or budget allocated to it. They sell hardware and software. Change management is the customer's problem.

The gap is significant. In service environments — hospitality, healthcare — the frontline workforce is the customer's most important operational asset. A robotics deployment that is poorly communicated, poorly sequenced, or that creates friction for the existing workforce will fail regardless of how well the robot performs technically. Staff will route around it, find reasons to override it, and the utilisation rate will drop to near zero within months.

**What resolves this:** A deployment methodology that includes explicit change management phases — workforce briefings, task portfolio redesign, supervisor training, and an escalation path that frontline staff control. The robot is not presented as a replacement. It is introduced as a colleague with a specific job description, and the human's job is enhanced — not diminished — by the redistribution of tasks.

## Barrier 3: The data does not exist yet

Modern robotics systems improve through operational data. The perception models that enable navigation, object recognition, and exception handling improve as they are exposed to more examples from the real environment.

This creates a fundamental procurement challenge: the performance an enterprise wants to buy does not yet exist at the point of purchase. The vendor can demonstrate performance in a representative environment, but the robot's performance in the specific enterprise facility — with its specific floor textures, lighting conditions, obstacle patterns, and workflow variations — will be lower at deployment and improve over time.

Enterprises that understand this and plan for it can structure a deployment that builds toward the target performance over a defined period. Enterprises that treat the demo as the delivery standard will be disappointed from day one and will struggle to maintain organisational commitment through the improvement period.

The deeper issue: generating the training data that drives improvement requires operational time and structured feedback from the facility's own staff. Enterprises need to be prepared to invest in this — labelling exceptions, confirming correct behaviour, and flagging errors — as an operational activity during the ramp-up period. Vendors who do not provide tooling for this, or who do not include it in the deployment plan, are setting their customers up for a plateau rather than an improvement curve.

**What resolves this:** Honest pre-deployment performance baselining, a structured supervised learning phase with explicit tooling for staff feedback, and a deployment contract that is structured around performance milestones rather than hardware delivery.

## Barrier 4: IT and OT security review is a genuine blocker

A robot is an IoT device on the enterprise network. It carries cameras, microphones, network interfaces, and in some cases biometric sensing. It communicates with enterprise systems that contain sensitive operational and customer data.

Enterprise IT and security teams are not wrong to treat this carefully. The review process for a networked robotic device in a healthcare or hospitality environment can take six to twelve months — longer in regulated industries. Many robotics vendors, optimised for speed-to-deployment, have not built the documentation, certifications, and security architecture that this review requires.

Data sovereignty is a particular concern in Singapore and the broader Asia-Pacific region. Enterprises need to know where operational data is stored, who can access it, and what happens to it when the contract ends. A cloud-dependent robotics platform with data processed outside the jurisdiction creates regulatory and contractual risk that procurement teams cannot accept.

**What resolves this:** On-premise or hybrid deployment options, documented security architecture, and data sovereignty compliance built into the product — not retrofitted as a response to a customer's security questionnaire.

## The common thread

Every barrier above shares the same underlying cause: robotics vendors have been optimised to win pilots, not to deliver enterprise-scale deployments. The pilot environment is controlled, the integration is minimal, the workforce impact is limited, and the security review is deferred. The real barriers only appear when the organisation tries to move from one robot in one area to ten robots across an operation.

The enterprises that will move forward are the ones that find vendors who have already solved these problems — not for pilots, but for operations.
